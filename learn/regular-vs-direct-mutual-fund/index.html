Regular vs Direct Mutual Fund:
The ₹34 Lakh Difference
Nobody Tells You About

Every mutual fund in India exists in two identical versions — same fund manager, same portfolio, same stocks. One version costs you ₹34 lakh more over 20 years. The reason is hidden in three words: Total Expense Ratio.

Netara Research · December 2024 · 8 min read · Updated for FY25 TER norms
Key takeaways
  • A regular plan and a direct plan of the same mutual fund hold identical assets — the only difference is cost.
  • Regular plans deduct 0.5–1.2% annually as distributor trail commission, silently, before your NAV is calculated.
  • On ₹25L invested for 20 years at 12% gross, the gap between regular and direct is ₹34 lakh in terminal wealth.
  • 78% of Indian MF AUM is still in regular plans — most investors do not know they have a choice.
  • Switching is taxable. This article tells you exactly when switching makes sense and when it does not.

What is the actual difference?

In January 2013, SEBI mandated that every mutual fund scheme in India offer two separate plans: a Regular Plan and a Direct Plan. Both plans invest in exactly the same securities, managed by the same fund manager, following the same investment mandate.

The only structural difference is this: in a regular plan, the AMC pays your distributor — your bank, broker, or mutual fund platform — a trail commission every year. This commission is not billed to you separately. It is quietly deducted from the fund's Net Asset Value (NAV) each day, 1/365th of the annual rate, before the number you see on your screen is calculated.

In a direct plan, no distributor is involved. The trail commission disappears. The full gross return of the fund stays in your account.

Same fund. Same portfolio. Same manager. Different NAV. The gap compounds against you every single day.

The compounding math — in rupees, not percentages

Most articles on this topic stop at "direct plans have a lower expense ratio." That is technically correct but structurally dishonest — it obscures how severe the compounding effect is over time. Let us do the actual math.

Assumption: ₹25,00,000 invested in a large-cap equity fund. Gross returns: 12% CAGR. Regular plan TER: 1.3%. Direct plan TER: 0.2%. Net return in regular plan: 10.7%. Net return in direct plan: 11.8%.

The gap in Year 1 is ₹27,000 — noticeable, but easy to dismiss. By Year 20 it is ₹34 lakh. This is not a linear cost. It is a compounding tax on your patience, growing every year regardless of how the market performs.

Notice also that the absolute rupee gap accelerates: it takes 10 years to reach ₹6.68 lakh, but only another 10 years to add another ₹27.66 lakh on top of that. The later years are by far the most expensive years to stay in a regular plan.

Why does your bank still put you in regular plans?

This is the structural question most articles avoid. Your bank, your broker, and your mutual fund platform earn the trail commission from your regular plan. As long as you stay invested, they receive 0.5–1.2% of your AUM annually — without any additional work on their part. It is passive income for them, deducted invisibly from your returns.

This creates a direct conflict of interest. A bank relationship manager who tells you to switch to a direct plan is eliminating his own revenue source. The incentive architecture makes honest advice structurally unlikely — not because the RM is dishonest, but because the system is not designed for your benefit.

According to ACE MF data, the expense ratio difference between direct and regular plans for all equity-oriented schemes ranged from 30 to 190 basis points in April 2025 — with a median gap near 1% for large and mid-cap equity funds. That gap exists entirely to pay distribution commissions.

SEBI has been aware of this structural misalignment for years. The introduction of direct plans in 2013 was precisely intended to give investors a commission-free option. ₹68 trillion of Indian mutual fund industry AUM has been accumulated since — yet the majority remains in regular plans, because the default recommendation from every bank and broker is a regular plan.

Which fund categories have the highest regular-to-direct gap?

The trail commission gap is not uniform across fund categories. It is highest where the distributor's perceived "value" justifies a larger fee — and lowest for passive funds where the argument for active management collapses.

The practical implication: if you are in a regular-plan small-cap or mid-cap fund, the annual drag is largest. A ₹10L position in a regular small-cap fund at 1.2% trail gap costs you ₹12,000 per year — before compounding.

Conversely, index funds have the smallest gap because their total TER is already minimal. The argument for switching an index fund from regular to direct is weaker than for an active equity fund — though still valid.

Should you switch? The honest answer.

The mathematically correct answer is: almost always yes, for equity funds held for more than 2–3 years. But there are important caveats.

Switching is taxable. Switching from a regular plan to a direct plan is treated as a redemption followed by a fresh investment. If you have unrealised capital gains, you will crystallise a tax liability at the point of switch. For equity funds held under 12 months: STCG tax of 20%. For equity funds held over 12 months: LTCG tax of 12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh in gains per financial year. Factor this into your decision — especially for large, long-held positions.

When switching clearly makes sense

You are in a regular large-cap or mid-cap fund, you have held it for more than 12 months (so LTCG applies at 12.5%, not STCG at 20%), and your unrealised gain is modest. The annual TER saving of 0.8–1.1% will recoup the one-time tax cost within 1–3 years, after which you are structurally better off every year.

When to think carefully before switching

You have very large unrealised gains (many years of appreciation) and selling would trigger a significant LTCG liability. In this case, calculate: years-to-recoup = tax-payable ÷ (annual-TER-saving × current-corpus). If that number exceeds 5–6 years, the switch may not be worth it for this specific fund — though stopping fresh SIPs into the regular plan and redirecting them to direct is costless and should be done immediately regardless.

The easiest action: stop new SIPs in regular plans

Even if you decide not to switch existing holdings due to tax considerations, you should never start a new SIP in a regular plan. New investments have zero embedded gains — there is no tax cost to switching a fresh investment to direct. Every rupee invested into a regular plan from today forward starts accumulating trail commission drag from Day 1.

How to switch from regular to direct — step by step

1

Identify your regular plan holdings

Log into CAMS or KFintech using your PAN and email. Download your consolidated account statement. Every fund with "Regular" or "Reg" in its name is costing you trail commission.

2

Calculate the tax liability before acting

For each holding: current value minus original investment = unrealised gain. Apply 12.5% LTCG (if held 12+ months) or 20% STCG (if held under 12 months). Know this number before you initiate any switch.

3

Check exit loads

Most equity funds charge a 1% exit load if redeemed within 12 months of purchase. Confirm your holding period. Switching before 12 months compounds your tax cost with an exit load — wait until month 12 if possible.

4

Redeem from regular plan and invest in direct

Go directly to the AMC's website (HDFC Mutual Fund, SBI MF, Mirae Asset, etc.) or use a direct-plan-only platform such as MF Central. Select the fund, choose "Direct Plan" at the scheme selection step. The NAV of the direct plan will be higher — that is correct and expected.

5

Cancel regular plan SIPs, start direct plan SIPs

Do not forget this step. A common error is switching existing holdings but leaving SIPs running into the regular plan. Cancel each regular SIP from the platform where it was set up, then re-create it on the AMC's direct portal or MF Central.

The regular plan is not always wrong — here is the honest case for it

Every article on this topic presents the direct plan as an unambiguous winner. That is almost always true, but intellectual honesty requires acknowledging when a regular plan is reasonable.

A regular plan makes sense if you genuinely benefit from the advisor's guidance — if the advisor actively helps you avoid panic-selling during a market crash, rebalances your portfolio, or prevents you from chasing momentum. Behavioural coaching has real financial value. Regular plans offer personalised guidance and convenience, and for new investors or those lacking time to manage investments, the slightly higher expense ratio may be worthwhile for this support.

The problem is that most investors in regular plans are not receiving meaningful, ongoing advisory services. They bought a fund once, on the recommendation of a bank RM, and have been paying trail commission every year since — without any follow-up advice, portfolio review, or rebalancing. In that case, you are paying premium pricing for a service you are not receiving.

The test: When did your distributor last call you to suggest a change to your portfolio? If the answer is "never" or "only to sell me something new" — you are paying for advice and receiving distribution. Those are not the same thing.

Frequently asked questions

Is the direct plan NAV always higher than the regular plan NAV?
Yes — always, for any fund that has been in existence for more than a day. Since the direct plan deducts less from NAV each day (no trail commission), its NAV compounds faster. The older the fund, the larger the NAV difference. A higher NAV does not mean the fund is more expensive — it means it has retained more value per unit.
Can I invest in direct plans through my existing bank account?
Most private banks (HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Axis Bank) sell only regular plans through their investment portals — this is how they earn trail commission. To invest in direct plans, you need to go to the AMC's website directly, use MF Central, or use a direct-plan-only platform such as Zerodha Coin or Kuvera. Your existing bank account can still be used for payment — you are only changing the investment portal, not your bank.
Does switching from regular to direct reset the LTCG holding period?
Yes. Switching is treated as a fresh purchase. Your LTCG holding clock resets to zero from the date of the new investment in the direct plan. If you are close to the 12-month mark on an existing holding, it may make sense to wait until the holding period crosses 12 months before switching, to lock in the lower LTCG rate rather than paying STCG.
Are there any funds where the regular and direct TER gap is negligible?
Index funds and ETFs have the smallest gap — often only 0.1–0.2%. For a ₹10L investment in a Nifty 50 index fund, the annual difference between regular and direct may be ₹1,000–2,000. Still worth switching, but the urgency is lower compared to active equity funds where the gap can be ₹8,000–12,000 per ₹10L per year.
What is the total amount paid as trail commissions by Indian MF investors annually?
Based on AMFI AUM data for October 2024 and estimated average trail commission rates, Indian retail MF investors pay approximately ₹25,000–30,000 crore annually in trail commissions embedded in regular plan TERs. This amount does not appear on any statement — it is deducted from returns before they are calculated and reported.

The bottom line

The regular vs direct mutual fund choice is one of the most consequential financial decisions most Indian investors never consciously make. It was made for them — by the bank that onboarded them, the RM who recommended a fund, or the app that defaulted to regular plans because that is how the app earns revenue.

The math is not ambiguous. On a ₹25 lakh equity portfolio held for 20 years, the gap between regular and direct is approximately ₹34 lakh. That is not a rounding error. It is a second portfolio.

The action is straightforward: identify your regular plan holdings, calculate the tax cost of switching, and move to direct plans where the economics make sense. For any new investment, there is no argument for starting in a regular plan — the direct plan is available in the same fund, at a lower cost, with identical risk and identical underlying assets.

The only question left is whether the distributor who put you in a regular plan is providing ₹34 lakh worth of value over the next 20 years. In most cases, the honest answer is no.
